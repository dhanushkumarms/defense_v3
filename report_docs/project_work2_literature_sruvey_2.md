# **1\. Introduction**

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant capabilities in various natural language processing tasks. However, their vulnerability to adversarial prompts, paraphrased harmful instructions, and jailbreak attacks presents serious safety concerns. These attacks exploit weaknesses in safety alignment by preserving malicious intent while modifying the surface structure of prompts. In our previous implementation, a semantic smoothing-based defense mechanism was used to mitigate such attacks. When evaluated on a dataset consisting of 7500 prompts categorized into direct harmful prompts, simple paraphrased prompts, and adversarial jailbreak prompts, the defense achieved Defense Success Rate (DSR) of 73%. Although this shows partial robustness, a significant portion of adversarial prompts were still able to bypass the defense.

The primary limitations of the baseline defense include insufficient diversity in semantic transformations, simplistic aggregation of safety decisions, lack of independent safety verification, and absence of early intent detection. These limitations reduce the effectiveness of the defense against sophisticated adversarial attacks.

Recent research suggests that improving semantic diversity, using intelligent aggregation strategies, incorporating independent safety classifiers, and adopting layered defense architectures can significantly enhance robustness. This section reviews existing literature supporting these defense upgrades.

# **2\. Literature Survey**

# **2.1 Diverse Semantic Neighborhood Generation**

One of the major weaknesses of basic semantic smoothing defenses is the limited diversity of generated semantic transformations. If the transformations are too similar to the original prompt in structure or wording, adversarial intent may remain hidden and undetected. Attackers often use paraphrasing, indirect phrasing, or role-based prompts to disguise harmful intent while preserving meaning. Beddiar et al. demonstrated that combining backtranslation and paraphrasing techniques generates semantically equivalent yet structurally diverse text, which improves the robustness of NLP models against adversarial inputs \[1\]. Wang et al. showed that augmentation methods such as synonym substitution, lexical replacement, and paraphrasing enhance resistance to adversarial inputs by exposing models to multiple semantic variations \[2\]. Li et al. further confirmed that paraphrase diversification expands the semantic coverage around the original prompt, improving the detection of hidden adversarial intent and strengthening defense performance \[3\].

These studies highlight that generating diverse semantic variants allows the defense system to analyze a wider range of prompt interpretations, thereby improving the effectiveness of semantic smoothing.

# **2.2 Confidence-Weighted Aggregation**

Another limitation of baseline defenses is the use of simple aggregation techniques such as majority voting, which treat all predictions equally regardless of their confidence level. In practice, some predictions may be more reliable than others, and ignoring confidence levels can lead to incorrect final decisions. Kuncheva et al. demonstrated that weighted voting improves classification accuracy by assigning higher importance to more reliable predictions, resulting in better overall performance \[4\]. Similarly, Zhang et al. showed that adaptive ensemble methods improve robustness by intelligently combining prediction outputs based on confidence scores and reliability \[5\].

These findings support the use of confidence-weighted aggregation, which enables the defense system to make more accurate and reliable safety decisions.

# **2.3 External Safety Classifiers**

Baseline defenses rely mainly on the internal safety alignment of LLMs, which can sometimes fail when exposed to adversarial prompts or jailbreak attacks. This creates the need for an independent safety verification mechanism. Dinan et al. proposed content moderation systems that utilize external classifiers to detect harmful or unsafe outputs generated by language models \[6\]. Mozafari et al. developed BERT-based toxicity detection models capable of accurately identifying harmful content such as toxic or unsafe language \[7\].

These studies demonstrate that external safety classifiers provide an additional layer of protection by independently verifying the safety of generated responses, thereby improving overall defense effectiveness.

# **2.4 Intent Risk Classification**

Early detection of harmful intent is an important strategy for improving system safety. Intent classification methods analyze the user prompt and determine whether it contains malicious or harmful intent before further processing. Xu et al. showed that intent classification techniques can accurately detect harmful prompts based on semantic and linguistic features \[8\]. Alhuzali et al. demonstrated that early harmful intent detection improves conversational AI safety by preventing unsafe prompts from reaching downstream components \[9\].

These findings justify the use of intent classifiers as an initial defense layer to detect and block direct harmful prompts.

# **2.5 Layered Defense Architecture**

Single-layer defenses are often insufficient to handle sophisticated adversarial attacks, as attackers may exploit weaknesses in individual defense mechanisms. Zhang et al. emphasized that layered defense architectures provide superior protection by combining multiple safety mechanisms into a unified framework \[10\]. Multi-stage defense pipelines can address different types of attacks more effectively, improving overall robustness.

These studies support the adoption of layered defense strategies, where multiple defense mechanisms work together to improve LLM safety.

**3\. Conclusion**

This literature review highlights key strategies for improving the robustness of LLM defense mechanisms. The baseline semantic smoothing defense achieved 73% accuracy when tested on 7500 adversarial prompts, indicating the need for improvement. Research shows that diverse semantic transformations improve detection of adversarial prompts, confidence-weighted aggregation enhances decision accuracy, external safety classifiers provide independent verification, and intent classifiers enable early detection of harmful inputs. Furthermore, layered defense architectures offer comprehensive protection against multiple attack types.

These findings provide strong justification for upgrading the defense mechanism to improve overall safety performance.
